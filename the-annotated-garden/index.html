The Annotated Garden

Workshop w/ Renée Turner @ SNV, Rotterdam

What does a city garden tell us about Realities in Transition?

Walking the meticulously organized plot grid of SNV, city garden in Rotterdam West, it shows immediately its accurate distribution, proof of a collective coexistence. SNV exists as a community of multiple and diverse cultures of gardeners meeting in a wetland squeezed between the Blijdorp Zoo and the Rotterdam-Delft highway. Founded in 1938, over its lifetime the city garden underwent a few conflicts with the Rotterdam municipality threatening its existence; it has always been readily protected by the same collectively organized gardeners.

Hacking tiny ecosystems

The discipline of gardening, in its playful form of “hacking” tiny ecosystems, allows the gardener to participate in and intervene within the natural cycles of plants, insects, moss, fungi, flowers, fruits, vegetables, worms, weather conditions and their entangled intrications. Gardening, in its playful, human, immediate scale, teaches us something about wider, slower, inaccessible ecosystems: that they exist through the ties and interdependencies of their many single elements.

Gardening as playful tinkering

As an amateur technologist—meaning I lack a certified education in computer science, yet I practice it daily as a hobby—I consider the pedagogy of technologies to be a playground that allows playful understandings of otherwise complex and inaccessible systems. What drives my discipline is not only accessibility, in the form of FL/OSS and low-tech communities, but safety to experiment and no-endpoint approaches.

Playing around with a cheap ultrasonic Arduino sensor in the attempt to build a lo-fi self-driving toy car, with no real applied purpose, teaches me something about autonomous systems’ ways of sensing. I do not cause damage to anyone if my car does not pass the Trolley dilemma. This safe-to-play approach also exists in the garden/forest analogy. When we garden, we participate and intervene within the same life/death cycles of nature that are found in a hostile forest, or an inhospitable ocean floor, yet our gardens do not pose similar threats to our life.

Literacies and imaginaries

Low-tech and gardening disciplines are both DIY/hobbyist portals to wider understandings of complex systems. There are already numerous analogies between nature and digital technology, including literary terms borrowed from one another such as web, cloud, bug, virus, and tree. These literacies simplify and often displace the popular perception of how complex systems work. Yet borrowing terms from floral ecologies allows for something that is by nature the most playful, safe, and purposeless practice: imagining.

Re-ecologizing Techs

When we imagine data collection in AI as a process of “composting” (gardenreadings.net/florian), we take an imaginary leap into how the ecological consequences of composting transpose to the ecosystem of an AI dataset.

We fidget around with meanings such as shit, feces, waste, scrap, but also energy, matter, feed, and fertilize. This imaginary game allows us to intervene in the rooted literacies of AI, and to rethink and re-ecologize their systems. The urgent question is: what metaphors and parables can we use to re-imagine alternative ecologies of technological systems? To answer this, we need to first question what alternative ecologies we want, need, or care about.

Ecologies of Care

Care is an inherent process in disciplines of gardening. It is what determines the life or death of delicate strawberry plants that need timely water and good exposure to sunlight. Care, here, is defined in contrast with indifference or exploitation. Care is to affect and maintain with concern and cooperation, rather than disinvolvement; to not retreat, flee, or withdraw from a system, but rather to stick in, remain, and grow-with.

When thinking of ecologies of care in gardening, we think of direct acts like watering our mimosas, greenhousing our lettuces, trimming the bush, moderating invasive plants, and feeding the soil. When we transpose that to digital technologies, let us ask: how would watering our software look? How do we greenhouse our digital lettuces? Trim our big files? Moderate invasive cookies? Feed the soil of our pixels?

Scales

Something I often recur to, when thinking of interdisciplinary analogies, is the concept of scale. Not only in its spatial undertone, but as a dimensional measure of things: size, time, and diverse ranges of the two.

When we look at the SNV garden from an online satellite map, we appreciate it differently. We see its real size and get a glimpse of the bisons in the zoo next door, and the cars on the highway. The different scale in which we see it triggers a sensation of awe, because it is not a measure directly accessible to our eyes. A similar sense of awe happens when we look at microscopic images of bacteria living on the tip of a leaf, or timelapse videos of growing trees and rotting fruits. More-than-human scales of seeing.

Conclusion

This replacement of scales often assists us in understanding ecosystems we would not resonate with otherwise; things we would and could not normally care about. For this reason, scale should be included in the problem of transposing ecologies of care to technological systems. Not only should we ask what metaphors and analogies we need, but also: what new scales of perception can ecology bring into the realm of the digital?

Realities in Transition is a concept that uses two extremely ecological terms: Realities, a plural word denoting multiplicities of being, and Transition, implying impermanence, in the same way that the life of an oak tree implies its own death, regardless of how eternal the tree’s cycle can seem in human years.

My father, a former software developer, started gardening as a lockdown hobby. In 2020 he began farming 1kg of redworms. Now, our backyard in Campodarsego, Italy, welcomes 50kg in batches of diverse shapes and materials. He tells me with excitement how inspiring the societal organizations of redworms are: they are androgynous, meaning they can mate with any other redworm; they multiply fast but never saturate a batch.

While my father jokes that redworms save him waste taxes, because they eat biodegradable waste, even pizza cartons, he also shows how this practice gave him a deeper understanding of recycling processes. Then he shows me the YouTube account and the subreddit where he got all the technical knowledge from.

In a small scale, as big as a house backyard, a DIY gardener discovers the hidden ecosystems of life generated from waste. This process of discovery does not remove itself from care, affection and maintenance. It is reinforced by them. We can learn Realities in Transition with the same passionate hobbyist pedagogy: a pedagogy that is interdisciplinary, metaphorical, scaled out, and guided by ecological care.
